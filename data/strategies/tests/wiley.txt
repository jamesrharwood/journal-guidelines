            <h2 class="section__mainHeader--mid">
                On the Cover
            </h2>
        
        <div class="slideShow"><a href="#afterSlideshow-nes" class="sr-only-focusable">Skip slideshow</a><div data-loop="true" data-items="1" data-slideby="1" data-autoheight="false" data-responsive="" data-speed="5000" data-autoplay="true" data-animation="slide" data-indicators="true" data-arrow="true" data-controls="true" data-stagePadding='0' data-label="Slideshow" class="owl-carousel" data-pause-slide="Pause slideshow" data-play-slide="Play slideshow" data-go-to-slide="Go to slide:" data-next-slide="Go to next slide" data-prev-slide="Go to previous slide"><div class="slide-item">



        
        <div class="slide-item">
<div class="creative-work left-side-image"><a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202000979"><img src="/pb-assets/assets/16121880/cover/slide-1-1620160687677.jpg" alt="Front Cover: Synthesis, Biological Evaluation, and Docking Study of Ring-Opening Amide Analogs of Matrine as Antitumor Agents (4/2021)" title="Front Cover: Synthesis, Biological Evaluation, and Docking Study of Ring-Opening Amide Analogs of Matrine as Antitumor Agents (4/2021)"></a></div>
<div><b>Front Cover</b>. Matrine, an alkaloid extracted from the roots of <i>Sophora flavescens</i>, which has been widely studied as a potential anti-cancer agent against various cancers. However, the activity of matrine is limited because of its low efficacy and short half-life. To overcome these disadvantages, two series of matrine analogues with ring-opening in the lactam portion of the molecule were designed and synthesized. Our <i>in vitro</i> cytotoxicity study showed that analogue B11 with a <i>meta</i>-bromide on the phenyl ring displayed the best antiproliferative activity. Moreover, B11 induced cell cycle arrest in G1 phase and cell apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner in A549 cells. Molecular modeling revealed that B11 achieved a higher docking score compared to its precursor A11 and parent compound matrine, possibly because B11 formed a hydrogen bond with SER91 and a halogen bond with GLN320 on the binding site of annexin A2 as reported by <i>Qian et al</i>. in their
full paper at <a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202000979">10.1002/cbdv.202000979</a>.</div>
</div>


</div><div class="slide-item">



        
        <div class="slide-item">
<div class="creative-work left-side-image"><a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202000992"><img src="/pb-assets/assets/16121880/cover/slide-2-1620160688643.jpg" alt="Front Cover: Argireline: Needle-Free Botox as Analytical Challenge (Chem. Biodiversity 3/2021)" title="Front Cover: Argireline: Needle-Free Botox as Analytical Challenge (Chem. Biodiversity 3/2021)"></a></div>
<div><b>Front Cover</b>. Creams and other cosmetics are often applied to reduce the visible ageing effects, such as facial wrinkles. Argireline, an acetyl hexapeptide Ac-Glu-Glu-Met-Gln-Arg-Arg-NH<sub>2</sub>, sometimes described as &lsquo;needle-free Botox' is often added to higher-end anti-wrinkle cosmetic products. However, detection and quantification of argireline content in commercially available cosmetics is complicated due to the low concentration and very complex matrix. The application of such sensitive method as LC-MS may not be enough to detect argireline in some products, moreover, the experiments revealed that the methionine residue is often oxidized, which may affect its biological properties in unexpected and as of yet uninvestigated ways, as reported by <i>Kluczyk et al</i>. in their full paper at <a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202000992">10.1002/cbdv.202000992</a>.</div>
</div>


</div><div class="slide-item">



        
        <div class="slide-item">
<div class="creative-work left-side-image"><a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202000883"><img src="/pb-assets/assets/16121880/cover/slide-3-1620160689387.jpg" alt="Front Cover: Investigation of the Effects of Primary Structure Modifications within the RRE Motif on the Conformation of Synthetic Bovine Herpesvirus 1-Encoded UL49.5 Protein Fragments (Chem. Biodiversity 2/2021)" title="Front Cover: Investigation of the Effects of Primary Structure Modifications within the RRE Motif on the Conformation of Synthetic Bovine Herpesvirus 1-Encoded UL49.5 Protein Fragments (Chem. Biodiversity 2/2021)"></a></div>
<div><b>Front Cover</b>. TAP transporter is a key component of the MHC class I-restricted antigen presentation pathway, shuttling antigenic peptides across the ER membrane. BoHV-1 herpesvirus-encoded UL49.5 protein is a viral TAP inhibitor. Searching for the molecular mechanism of UL49.5 action, variations in the RRE(9-11) motif of its N-terminal domain were designed to abolish (RRE(9-11)GGG) or stabilize (RRE(9-11)AAA and RRE(9-11)E11G) the structure of the &alpha;-helix. The desired structural alterations were confirmed by circular dichroism, NMR, and molecular dynamics in membrane-mimetic environments. The introduced RRE modifications slightly altered the biological activity of UL49.5 in human cells, as demonstrated by MHC I level measurements by flow cytometry; yet, they could not abolish the UL49.5-mediated peptide transport arrest. The results indicate that other structural motifs may contribute to TAP inhibition as reported by <i>Karska et al</i>. in their full paper at
<a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202000883">10.1002/cbdv.202000883</a>.</div>
</div>


</div><div class="slide-item">



        
        <div class="slide-item">
<div class="creative-work left-side-image"><a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202001004"><img src="/pb-assets/assets/16121880/cover/slide-4-1620160690320.jpg" alt="Cover Picture: Phosphonic Analogs of Alanine as Acylpeptide Hydrolase Inhibitors (Chem. Biodiversity 2/2021)" title="Cover Picture: Phosphonic Analogs of Alanine as Acylpeptide Hydrolase Inhibitors (Chem. Biodiversity 2/2021)"></a></div>
<div><b>Cover Picture</b>. The primary function of acylpeptide hydrolase is associated with the removal of N-acetylated amino acid residues from proteins and peptides. Considering the protein degradation cycle cross-talk between acylpeptide hydrolase and proteasome, inhibition of the first enzyme resulted in down-regulation of the ubiquitin-proteasome system and induction of cancer cell apoptosis as reported by <i>Maciej Walczak et al</i>. in their full paper at<a href="/doi/10.1002/cbdv.202001004">10.1002/cbdv.202001004</a>.</div>
</div>

Five new peptaibols, longibramides A  E (<b>1</b><b>5</b>) with 11 amino acid residues, were isolated from a fungus <i>Trichoderma longibrachiatum</i> Rifai DMG311, which was isolated from a mushroom <i>Clitocybe nebularis</i> (Batsch) P. Kumm collected from coniferous forest in the subboreal area of northeast China. The structures of longibramides A  E were determined by their spectroscopic data (NMR and MSMS spectra), their absolute configurations were determined by Xray diffractions and Marfey's analyses. The Xray diffractions of longibramides A, B and the similar CD spectra of A  E showed that they all had <i></i>helix conformations. Longibramides B and E showed moderate cytotoxicities against BV2 and MCF7 cells, and also showed some inhibitory effects against methicillinresistant <i>Staphylococcus aureus</i> MRSA T144. This result enriched the family of peptaibol, as reported by <i>Zhang et al</i>. in their full paper at<a href="https://doi.org/10.1002/cbdv.202100128">10.1002/cbdv.202100128</a>.
